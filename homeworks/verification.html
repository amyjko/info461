Verification Plans

Andrew J. Ko

The first software defect was reportedly discovered by Grace Hopper, a U.S. Navy Rural Admiral, while she was writing programs for the Harvard Mark II. She literally found a moth stuck between a relay in the machine, then taped it into the Mark II's log book with the explanation: "First actual case of bug being found." Now, long before that, there were defects in programs; defects are a part of developing software. We make mistakes, then we fix them.

What we don't do is ship them. No one wants to use software that doesn't do what it was intended to do. That defeats the core purpose of software: it does things reliably and fast.

What we do to prevent shipping defects is verify. But we have to do this in a way that actually decreases the likelihood that our users find defects. In every case, this means making a plan.

Because there are many ways to verify software, plans can vary. Some plans might be heavily focused on testing, whereas others might use formal verification. Others might just focus on shipping software and having users report failures. All of this depend what your team's tolerance for failure is.

Your job in this homework is to devise such a plan. Your plan should include:

  • A list of all of the requirements that you will verify. You don't have to verify them all (nor likely can you).
  • For each requirement that you will verify, a verification plan:
    • If you propose to write a tests, what exact tests will you conduct and what will count as each test passing? Will you write an automated test or run the test manually?
    • If you propose to conduct reviews or inspections, how will you analyze the code? If you write a proof, what property will you prove? If you conduct a review or inspection, what aspects of the code will you inspect to verify the requirement is met?
  • For all of the requirements, how will your verifications be integrated into your process? Will you run automated tests after every build? Before every commit? When will you conduct inspections and who will be involved?

The structure of your written should reflect your plans; since every plan is different, every written plan will be different.

Grading

This homework is worth more than previous ones because verification is key to achieving your requirements. We will read your reports and judge them according to the following single scale

  • 4 points. If implemented correctly, the plan has high likelihood of verifying major functional defects before shipping.
  • 3 points. If implemented correctly, the plan will identify most major functional defects before shipping, but some will be identified after shipping.
  • 2 points. If implemented correctly, the plan will identify some major functional defects before shipping, but many will be identified after shipping.
  • 1 points. Major parts of the plan are infeasible.
  • 0 points. The plan cannot be implemented or was not submitted.

In preparing your report, try to judge your plan against this scale yourself. How can you know if your plan will identify defects? Coverage is key. We will review each of your requirements and ensure that the verifications you propose for each will cover the various ways that requirement might be achieved. The lower the coverage, the lower the likelihood of finding defects.
