Homework 1: Design your organization (4 points)

Andrew J. Ko

The first step of any software project is defining why the project is happening at all. What problem is it solving? Why have the specific people on a team come together to solve it? What makes them the right people to solve it? Do they have the skills to solve it? In this homework, you're going to answer these questions, designing your organization intentionally.

Step 1: Creating a team (30 min)

Most teams come together around trust first and then choose a problem. This is because is trusting relationships are necessary for collaboration: they provide the psychological safety (Edmondson 1999) necessary for risk taking, feedback, and open communication. Because of this, we're going to form teams first, and then identify problems.

Your team must be 4 or 5 people. (I want the teams large enough that you encounter communication and coordination complexities that reflect real teams in practice). Because of the size of this class, you're not going to be able to form the exact team you might want. You're also going to have to interview each other to assess the potential for trusting relationships.

Here's the process to follow

  • If you already have a trusting relationship with someone in the class, team with them. This will provide a solid foundation.
  • Start interviewing classmates to select other teammates. Focus your questions on their motives, as teams that are working toward the same goals perform better than those that don't:
    • Why are you taking this class?
    • What time do you have to contribute to this class?
    • What skills do you have (and do they complement ours)?

Remember that this is a hiring marketplace. You may need to adopt some aggressive recruiting practices to land the candidates you need to succeed.

Once you have a team, write your names down on the team registration sheet for credit for today.

Step 2: Write a mission statement (10 min)

Your team needs a reason for being. What is it's overarching goal and it's overarching values? Are you trying to make the world a safer place? A healthier place? Are you just trying to get a high grade in this class? Choosing a larger purpose will focus everyone's work in the same direction and help resolve disagreements about how to spend your time, because every bit of work will serve different goals more or less. Write down your mission statement, arguing about the words as much as you need to until you come to agreement.

Step 3: Choose a problem to solve

Ideate problems you can solve within the scope of your mission statement. Remember that problems are not solutions—they are characterization of consequential causality. Solutions are what you make to change the causality of a problem so that it's consequences no longer occur.

I fully expect this step to take more time than we have in class. Discuss, debate, and deliberate outside of class to arrive at a problem you're all excited about solving this quarter.

Because we'll have limited time in this class to build, here are a few constraints on the problem you choose:

  • It should be a small problem, not requiring a massive, complex solution.
  • It should be addressable with web-based software
  • It does not have to be novel.

Step 4: Assign roles

This is a critical step, as it ensures at least one person is responsible for every important function of a team.

  • Who is responsible for marketing? This person will do all tasks that involve disseminating what you build outside of your team.
  • Who is responsible for product management? This person has the final say in what you're building, owning the requirements of the product.
  • Who is responsible for design? This person has the final say in how product requirements are met.
  • Who is responsible for engineering? These people are responsible for meeting product requirements with code.
  • Who is responsible for support? This person receives feedback from people outside your team and disseminates the feedback internally.

Remember that one person can take on multiple roles (and given your team sizes, each of you probably should take on multiple roles). Choose people who have the skills for the role or are eager to learn the skills for the role.

Step 5: Create a shared workspace

  • Create a GitHub Organization to represent your team (for it to be free, you'll need to make it open source).
  • Write your mission statement in the organization's description.
  • Ensure everyone on your team has access to the organization
  • Create a repository to store your project work and give everyone access
  • Give your your instructor (GitHub "andyjko") and your TA (GitHub "bxie") read access to the repository for grading purposes
  • In your repository's README, write your problem statement (defining what the goals of the repository) and write a list of your teammates and their responsibilities.

Grading Criteria

For activity credit, write your names down on the team registration sheet.

For homework credit, submit a link to your organization on Canvas.

Your shared GitHub space will be graded on the following scale:

  • 0.5 points for a public GitHub organization
  • 0.5 points for a public GitHub repository
  • 1.0 points for a mission statement with no spelling or grammar issues.
  • 2.0 points for a clear, concise, logically sound problem statement that defines the problem being solved. We will deduct points for statements that focus on solutions instead of problems.
